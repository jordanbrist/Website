Materials

11-7-2023

For the past two days I have been spending a lot of my time trying to figure out materials and textures to make the house look miniature, like a doll house.

Of course, a lot of this will come down to the camera, but the textures matter more than most people think. For instance, a wooden door may look like it is made of wood, but to create an appropriate sense of scale, the texture itself needs to be scaled properly and it needs to interact with the light in a way that it looks like it is made of real materials. Also, following a re-review of Mad Monster Party, I have been thinking more about the materials that would be common or easily available for production.





Modeling: House

11-5-2023

At first I started modeling an old car but soon realized that the amount of time that goes into making a car is exponentially greater than I should be spending on something that serves only as a background object with no real meaning.
Thus, the house is born...
Over many hours spanning the last three days, I have been working on the model for a house that will act as an integral part of the story. This house is a location from a scene in which the main character looks in and sees what appears to be a happy family. Children are playing and reminding him of a time that has long since passed and it serves as a way to ground the entire animation to a living world, rather than a setting devoid of other people or interaction, even though it is seen through the lens of one that can only view, not take part.

To start, I scavenged the internet for photos of mid-century houses. I eventually came upon one with this nice, big window looking in. It had a floorplan attached to it which I modified to fit my intended vision.
After That, I took the modified floorplan and created walls, a floor, a door, siding, windows, a porch, a roof, and more. It's not perfect, but it is coming along nicely.

I'm not sure just how much of the house will be visible in the final scene, so I opted to focus on the front and left side of the facade.





Mad Monster Party: Rethinking My Approach

10-31-2023

It's Halloween so, to get into the spirit, I decided to finally watch the 1967 Rankin Bass film "Mad Monster Party". I've seen screenshots and read some discussions about the movie before but this was the first time I've actually watched it. I have pages of notes...

Unlike many of the other Rankin Bass films, the sets and characters are (obviously) not visually tied to Christmas. This meant that the designs were more modern (for the time). I think that the setting and time-period being closer to the time period in which it was made was a better visual aid than the fantastical empty tundras of something like Rudolf the Red Nosed Reindeer. It allowed me to spend a bit more time focusing on the details of the background elements, the shot compositions, the character movements, the lighting, etc; to really have a better understanding of the direction for such a film.

One of the things that was most apparent, and also kind of ruined my current progress, is that the characters move in a way that looks jittery/jumpy but it isn't because they are shot on twos or threes. Nearly everything in this film appears to be animated at 24fps. This knowledge immediately makes me feel as though I wasted the last two weeks of, what I thought, was progress.
This means that, in order to achieve the desired look, I can't take any shortcuts. The thing that gives this style of animation its charm is the imperfections. The way a character walks kind of funnily or with a stride that is not always even or the same speed.
Another thing that stood out to me was this: not everything needs to move every frame but something needs to be moving every frame to make the shot feel alive.

I think that I need to step back from animating for a bit and at least make some props/sets that capture the visual style I want. Then, when I finally have a clue of what to do, I'll return to animating the character movements.





The Look is Almost There

10-18-2023

I exported this with a bitrate for Youtube specifically but it still doesn't look great. However, Vimeo apparently only allows users to post 2 videos each month without paying for a subscription so YouTube it is!

For this render I changed the motion interpolation on only the animation of the character rig to "stepped" for one frame which essentially holds the animation inbetween frames rather than transitioning between them on its own. The movement of the character's position/location in 3D space, along with the movement of the camera, is still moving linearly at 24fps. However, I did mess up when I exported this and the video above is actually playing at 12fps with the animation running at 6fps. When I look at the the one that is moving at the correct framerate, it actually still looks too smooth even though the character is moving at 12 fps. It could still use some further tweaking and exploration , but it is finally looking closer to the way I'd originally hoped.

After I go over it a bit more and finalize an approach for the movement/framerate I'll move on to the next step. That would be creating the rest of the sets (at least in a temporary capacity like this one) and setting up the camera and character movement so that they follow their intended paths. Then I can continue on the character animation. Right now I am using a walk cycle from Mixamo. I am planning on using some other animations from Mixamo as well, but will need to refine them and then do some hand animating for pretty much everything other than walking.

I still have those two models showing in the render because they currently work as a prop showing the difference in framerate between the character and the objects in the world.





FPS Testing

10-18-2023

I have spent the last couple of days trying out some different options for frame-rate. I started with 24 FPS and, of course, it looks too smooth. I then tried interpolating the position of each keyframe by two steps to make it choppier, but in actuallity it just ended up looking jittery. However, I did like the look of the animation moving at a different rate than the camera, which was still moving at 24 FPS. I tried scaling down the keyframes of the animation and then scaling them back up, which helped reduce the keyframe count, but the animation is still interpolating the positions of the rig on each frame which creates a smoothing effect. This current video is of a render in which I changed the render settings to animate everything at 12 FPS. I am currently rendering another pass of this test animation in which I change the frame interpolation to "stepped" only for the character so that it looks as though it is moving at a slower rate than the camera, but I made sure this time to keep the object motion as a linear path so that it won't move jerk forward on every second frame. This could mean that the animation will be moving slower than the movement of the character which would make things look strange/uncanny. If that ends up being the case then I will have to try it again and just increase the number of loops in the animation keyframes to match.





Render and Compositing Test

10-16-2023

YouTube's compression and bitrate makes things appear fuzzier than they should. Vimeo looks much better and closer to the actual rendered sequence. Apparently it could be due to the codec I chose when I exported.


I have changed my render engine, tweaked some settings, and then did some compositing on top of the rendered images. It is my first time doing any compositing in Blender so it took some time to figure out what exactly I needed to do but the node based editors aren't too different from Unreal Engine or TouchDesigner so it was a fairly straightforward process.

I added some film grain and scratches, a faint glow from light reflections, some lens distortion and a light jitter to mimic gate weave. At present, the film grain is too heavy and the gate weave is almost imperceptable. I think that with some minor adjustments and materials that mimic realistic textures things should begin to look the way I want.

As it stands, I am planning on animating each frame one-by-one (or two-by-two in this case for 12fps) to make the finished project look like stop-motion, but I will probably try out some add-ons or tricks to fake it or at least reduce the number of frames I'll need to animate by hand.





General Progress Update

10-15-2023

Over the course of this week I have refined the head sculpt, started some different attempts for the clothing (with and without cloth simulations), and tried out Blender's "hair curves" system of creating hair (not a fan of how it turned out, didn't look enough like it was the head of a puppet, more like real hair than I wanted, I'm going to try again using a community-made add-on). The character models will not be my main focus at this time, but I will be designing the rest of the characters soon.

Following all of that I realized that I should focus my efforts on the parts that are more important: rigging, set design, camera lens size and aperture (to capture the right look of a miniature set), and other details that will help sell the look of real objects. I also had to go through all of my render settings and set everything up so that things run more efficiently on my computer.

These pictures are from two cameras that I set up in the first mock-up of the first scene of my animation. Right now everything is just made from simple planes and cubes so that I can have a better understanding of the space I'll be working in. Once I have settled on scale and placement that feels "right" I will move on to the next scene and so forth until they are all set up properly. Once that is done I will begin modeling the sets with greater detail, using reference from screengrabs of Rankin-Bass films and other animated works by animator Tadahito Mochinaga.
